1/ South Korean content, exemplified by hits like Parasite, Squid Game, The Glory, Itaewon Class, and Mask Girl, has earned widespread global acclaim, underscoring the success of the country's entertainment industry.

2/ Despite this success, many directors and writers struggle financially. According to the Directors Guild of Korea, members earn an average of 18 million won (approximately $13.5K USD) per year.

3/ Creators, including directors and writers, often work on a freelance basis for production houses or studios, relinquishing intellectual property rights and future profits. Only established stars have the leverage to negotiate more favorable terms.

4/ Moreover, negotiations have become increasingly challenging due to the decline of traditional cinema and television platforms.

5/ Consequently, South Korean creators are advocating for changes to copyright laws to secure a "right to remuneration," although achieving this goal appears daunting in the short term.

6/ An interview cited in the article warns that without increased pay for creators, South Korean television and cinema may fade into obscurity, potentially driving young storytellers to pursue more lucrative opportunities such as webtoons.

7/ Directors and writers face their own set of challenges in South Korea's entertainment industry, highlighting the multifaceted nature of their profession.
